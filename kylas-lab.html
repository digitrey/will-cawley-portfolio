Introduction

This redesign focused on helping our client rebrand and create a better shopping experience for their store.

The Client

Kyla's Lab: Textile Arts & Accessories is an independent fiber dyer run out of Houston, Texas.

The owner, Kyla Crawford, is an expert in manipulating textile fibers through traditional and modern methods. She creates amazing and vibrant yarns for sale to individuals to use in their crafting projects.

The Problem

Despite these amazing creations, Kyla does not have a functioning online marketplace for her creations. It is completely empty.

We aimed to improve Kyla's website to improve her ability to sell yarn. In addition, we wanted to enable her to sell specialty classes. A fresh online space within her website could accomplish these goals while also creating an 'upsell' feedback loop.

User Research

To better understand Kyla's client base, we employed a mixture of quantitative and qualitative research methods.

Survey

Surveyed 498 participants across six popular yarn crafting Facebook groups for quantitative data.

At a Glance

Interviews

Interviewed three people with various yarn crafting skillsets for in-depth, qualitative data.

At a Glance

Affinity Diagram

The interview notes were transcribed and sorted into an affinity diagram to stect patterns.

Persona and Storyboard Formation

We used our data to inform the creation of a persona and storyboard. The persona guided our design decisions. The storyboard illustrated our ideal user flow.

Design and Ideation

Sketches

We began our redesign with a series of sketches. These low cost creations helped us think through the layout for the website without wasting too much time on a single idea.

We also experimented with the responsive design. The transition between desktop and mobile screens was thoroughly thought through.

Mid-Fidelity Designs

Once the main ideas were committed to paper, we debated the merits of each design. We then transferred these thoughts to Figma.

Each new screen was developed with their requirements thought out.

A-B Testing

At this stage of the process, we had two ideas for how to handle the shopping experience. Version A used an vertical scroll design. Version B used a horizontal scroll design. To decide between them, we tested with 3 potential users.

Our testers preferred vertical scrolling, but did not like how many options there were. So, we split the shopping into two columns to increase the amount of information on screen.

Lessons Learned

Future work for this project would include:

  • • A robust filtering process - Kyla's Lab does not need it now, but could use filtering when she has more items to sell in the future.
  • • Integrated content for online classes (message boards, PDF hosting, session sign-up, etc.)
  • • Newsletter/Blogging functionality
