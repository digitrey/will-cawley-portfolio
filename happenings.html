Introduction

In this design challange, we were given three weeks to define a problem and ideating possible solutions. Using the Design Thinking process, we proposed an application to help users organize social gatherings and allow guests to help with the planning.

The Challange

We began by conducting a Collaborative Sketch (C-Sketch) session to generate ideas. C-Sketching is a method for concept generation where designers create design concepts and then pass their sketches to another designer to modify. After all team members have been cycled through, they discuss and vote on the ideas to come to a consensus.

After our session, we decided on the seed idea of a 'drink mixing application.' This concept appeared to be the most relevant to our experiences and had the most energy behind it. There were also many directions we could go to differentiate the idea from other applications in the space beyond just alcholic beverages.

In fact, we had too many ideas. We needed to distill the concept down further to create a refined experience.

User Research

With our idea selected, we conducted user research to refine it. This research included both quantitative and qualitative methods. These methods and results are summarized below.

Survey

Short survey to gauge thoughts on gatherings and beverages.

Questions included:

  • • How often do you consume a beverage containing alcohol?
  • • When attending a gathering, who do you bring alcohol for?
  • • Which of the following ideas appeal to you?

Interviews

Long form interviews to find specific viewpoints and feelings.

Questions included:

  • • Are you an extrovert or an introvert?
  • • What role does alcohol play in your life?
  • • Do you prefer hosting gatherings or attending them?

Insights

After conducting our researech, we found that there was not much of an interest in mixing drinks at a party, and more interest creating an environment where guests feel comfortable and happy.

Essentially, people want to plan gatherings. However, they found it difficult to organize a cohesive party where everyone is having fun. With this insight in mind, we changed gears to take a holistic view of the party rather than the beverages alone.

Persona Formation

From our data, we created a persona. This persona guided our design decisions.

Design and Ideation

From Sketch to Figma

With our research complete, we got to work laying out a design for the main application, which we took to calling 'Happenings.'

Sketches

We started off with sketches. These low cost creations helped us think through potential layout ideas.

Figma Version

We then used Figma to mock-up a working version of the screen. These screens were strung together into a prototype.

User Workflows

We thought of two workflows to accomplish user goals on the assumption that some users would desire more options and input, while others would like a curated experience.

Wizard Flow

In the Wizard Flow, the host creates their gathering from provided options.



Random Flow

In the Random Flow, the host is given a ready-made gathering to reduce cognitive load.

User Testing

Unsure of which workflow was better, we conducted usability testing on the prototyped designs.

We conducted two flavors of usability testing.

  1. Usability Interviews - Longer, freeform conversations (4)
  2. 'Guerilla' Testing - Short, flow specific tests (5)

What Did We Find?

Many people preferred Wizard Flow over the Random Flow (6/9)

However testers did like aspects of the Random Flow:

  • • Summarization of choices
  • • Visually appealing format
  • • Customization potential

Future Work

If we were to continue developing the design, we take the following actions:

  • Hybrid Workflow — Combining our “Wizard” and “Random” Workflows to provide more customization options and use the visuals of the random flow.
  • Guest Experience — Build out the collaborative experience for guests.
  • High Fidelity Prototype — Create a 'pixel-perfect' creation to enable a developer to build the application.
